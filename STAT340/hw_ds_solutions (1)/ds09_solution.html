Part c: accounting for nonlinearity

Let’s see if we can improve our model. We know from physics class that kinetic energy grows like the square of the speed. Since stopping amounts to getting rid of kinetic energy, it stands to reason that stopping distance might be better predicted by the square of the speed, rather than the speed itself. It’s not exactly clear in the data that such a trend exists, but let’s try fitting a different model and see what happens.

Fit the model dist = beta0 + beta1*speed^2 to the cars data.

# carqm, q for quadratic
carqm <- lm(dist ~ 1 + I(speed^2), data=cars)

The residuals are approximately normally distributed according to the qq plot, and the variance seems constant, but there is a nonlinear pattern. We can consider a model with a squared term - parabolas can fit curves better. We can also try a log transformation of mpg.

A log transformation of mpg did the trick. The curve in the residual plot is largely gone and we have not introduced any other violations of the assumptions. We could write our new model as \[log(mpg) = 3.796 -.0014 hp - .18 wt -.0002disp\] Or by exponentiating both sides \[\hat{mpg} = \exp\{3.796 -.0014 hp - .18 wt -.0002disp \}\]
