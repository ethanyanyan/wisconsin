  1. Basketball player 1 makes 80% of their free throws and basketball players 2 makes 70% of their free throws. Assume all of their free throws attempts are independent. If each player takes ten free throws and \(X\) is the total number of free throws they make, does \(X\) have a binomial distribution? If yes, specify the parameters \(n\) and \(p\). If not, explain why not.

\(X\) does not have a binomial distribution. The total number of successful free throws \(X\) is the sum of the successful free throws from player 1 and player 2. While the number of successful free throws for each player separately does follow a binomial distribution, the distribution of \(X\) will be the convolution of two binomial distributions, not a single binomial distribution.

  • The two players have different probabilities of success, so we cannot use a single probability \(p\) for both.
  • Even though the trials are independent within each player’s attempts, the total number of successes \(X\) is the sum of two different binomial distributions, one for each player.

Thus, to find the distribution of \(X\), one would need to compute the sum of these two distributions. This sum does not simplify to a single binomial distribution because the probability of success differs between the two players.
